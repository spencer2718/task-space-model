\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}


Public discussion of automation and AI is surrounded by anxiety about job loss and wage stagnation. Important empirical questions include how to measure which parts of the labor market are exposed to technological change, how exposure propagates beyond an initial cluster of tasks, and which measurement choices survive falsifiable checks. Existing task-based exposure measures---routine versus non-routine, ``AI-exposed'' versus not---provide useful summaries but do not directly encode graded similarity across tasks, institutional barriers to worker reallocation, or spillovers from a shock that originates in one region of the task space and affects nearby tasks.


In principle, a sufficiently capable AI system with a correct structural model could simulate: new technologies as shocks in task space, reallocation paths of workers across occupations, and general-equilibrium wage and unemployment dynamics. Call this hypothetical system the \emph{task-space oracle}---a complete structural mapping $\mathcal{F}$ from task-space representations, technology shocks, and institutional structures to equilibrium labor-market outcomes.

This paper does not attempt to estimate $\mathcal{F}$, nor do we claim that validated components are sufficient for such an oracle; they are necessary conditions that candidate representations must satisfy. We treat economics as a method of model selection over candidate representations of tasks, frictions, and adjustment mechanisms.


The contribution is to develop and empirically test modular components of a future oracle: task-space geometry, institutional wedges, and mobility constraints. Each empirical test is a stress test of a specific module. If a candidate representation fails to predict observed labor market patterns, it is unlikely to be useful as an input to any downstream structural model. The framework earns its keep only if the resulting exposure indices are non-redundant relative to standard discrete baselines and if the task geometry passes non-tautological diagnostics.


A central theoretical contribution is the decomposition of effective distance between occupations into semantic and institutional components (\cref{subsec:decomposition}). Semantic distance captures task-content similarity: can the worker perform the tasks? Institutional distance captures credentialing and training barriers: is the worker allowed to perform the job? Two occupations may be semantically similar (e.g., brain surgeon and butcher both involve cutting) but economically distant due to vast differences in required credentials.


The primary empirical test uses worker-level occupation transitions from the Current Population Survey (CPS). In a conditional logit model of destination choice among 89,329 verified transitions, both semantic distance ($\alpha = 2.994$, $t = 98.5$) and institutional distance ($\beta = 0.215$, $t = 63.4$) are significant predictors of observed destinations. The large $t$-statistics primarily reflect sample size; we focus on effect magnitudes. On a standardized basis, the two effects are comparable: a one-standard-deviation increase in institutional distance is associated with 25\% lower odds of that destination, versus 19\% for semantic distance. The key finding is that both components are independently predictive ($r = 0.36$ between measures), not that one dominates.


A complementary validation using wage comovement across occupation pairs shows that the semantic geometry is statistically detectable: normalized kernel-weighted overlap achieves $R^2 = 0.00485$, compared to $R^2 = 0.00167$ for binary Jaccard overlap. Both values are small in absolute terms; the value of this test is discriminating between candidate representations, not explaining wage dynamics.

The paper's contribution is primarily descriptive: we show that semantic distance (task-content similarity) and institutional distance (job zone and certification differences) are both significant predictors of observed occupation transitions, with correlation $r = 0.36$ between the two measures. This decomposition is a necessary condition for any downstream structural use of task-space representations---if a representation fails to predict observed mobility, it is unlikely to be useful for counterfactual analysis. We do not claim to have built components of a future oracle; we validate candidate representations against observed patterns.


Finally, wage outcomes are conditional on institutions. Productivity effects need not pass through one-for-one into wages; wage-setting and pass-through environments can introduce occupation-specific wedges. We treat this as a scope condition (\cref{subsec:wedge}) and record a ``new tasks'' extension (\cref{subsec:newtasks}), while keeping the baseline empirical focus on evaluating exposure measurement.


\cref{sec:literature} reviews related literatures. \cref{sec:theory} develops the theoretical framework, including the oracle mapping, the semantic-institutional decomposition, and both continuous and discrete representations as alternatives to be tested. \cref{sec:empirics} operationalizes each component using O*NET data, reports the CPS mobility validation as the primary test and wage comovement as a complementary test, and specifies the prospective evaluation plan. \cref{sec:extensions} records extensions and historical regime signatures. \cref{sec:conclusion} concludes.


\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}


Research on automation and labor markets has increasingly adopted a task-based framework, analyzing how technologies complement or displace specific activities within occupations. These approaches can be understood as early attempts at low-dimensional approximations to a complete task-space oracle---coarse models that partition the task domain and predict differential effects across partitions.


In the routine-biased technological change (RBTC) paradigm, computerization disproportionately substitutes for routine, rule-based tasks while complementing non-routine analytic and interactive tasks \citep{AutorLevyMurnane2003}. Subsequent work formalized task assignment and emphasized that technological change acts on tasks, with equilibrium implications for employment reallocation and wages that cannot be recovered from a two-skill canonical model alone \citep{AcemogluAutor2011}. Empirically, this perspective underlies prominent exposure designs in which local labor markets more specialized in routine task work experienced larger subsequent declines in routine employment and a reallocation toward service work \citep{AutorDorn2013}, with cross-country evidence documenting related polarization patterns \citep{GoosManning2007}.


The canonical RBTC empirical methodology, established by \citet{AutorLevyMurnane2003} and extended by \citet{AutorDorn2013}, constructs task measures from the Dictionary of Occupational Titles (DOT) 1977 edition using five specific variables: GED-MATH (quantitative reasoning), DCP (direction/control/planning), STS (set limits/tolerances/standards), FINGDEX (finger dexterity), and EYEHAND (eye-hand coordination). These are aggregated into routine, abstract, and manual task indices, with the routine task intensity (RTI) index defined as the log-ratio $\text{RTI}_k = \ln(R_k) - \ln(M_k) - \ln(A_k)$. This methodology provides the benchmark against which continuous semantic measures can be tested. The key question is whether kernel-weighted task overlap with a routine shock profile predicts canonical outcomes better than discrete RTI-based classification.


The limitation, for measurement, is that many task-based implementations operationalize exposure through discrete partitions. Those partitions capture first-order patterns, but they obscure two objects that matter for reduced-form exposure regressions. First, within-category heterogeneity can be large: occupations classified as ``routine'' differ in how close their actual task bundles are to the frontier of a given technology. Second, substitution and complementarity are naturally graded: effects propagate most strongly to nearby tasks, not only to tasks that share a label. A potential contribution of this framework is to define a metric on the task/activity domain itself and represent occupations as distributions over that domain, so that proximity and overlap in task space determine exposure propagation.


This concern is not purely conceptual. Work using richer task detail documents economically meaningful variation within occupations and within broad task categories \citep{AutorHandel2013,SpitzOener2006}. Related evidence in the automation-risk literature shows that occupation-level exposure measures can substantially overstate the share of employment at high risk when they ignore within-occupation task heterogeneity \citep{ArntzGregoryZierahn2016}.


A separate literature studies worker mobility and occupational transitions. Structural labor and search models \citep[in the tradition of][]{Dorn2009} treat occupation-to-occupation transitions as revealing information about frictions and substitutability. If workers can easily move between two occupations, those occupations are economically close regardless of their linguistic descriptions. This motivates using observed mobility as a validation target for task-space geometry: a good representation should predict where workers actually go when they switch.


Historical episodes motivate the same object. Electrification illustrates how a general-purpose technology can shift the demand for task bundles in heterogeneous ways, with task-based evidence in early twentieth-century manufacturing consistent with reweighting across skill-relevant activities \citep{Gray2013}. Classic accounts emphasize the emergence of technology--skill complementarity under electrification through reorganized production, capital deepening, and the rising importance of skilled maintenance and management \citep{GoldinKatz1998,GoldinKatz2008}.


A separate literature studies exposure measurement in shift-share and related designs. The baseline empirical object is composition-predicted exposure: pre-period shares (industries, occupations, or tasks) interacted with common shocks. Work on Bartik instruments clarifies when such designs identify causal effects and how their weighting structure maps local exposure to national shocks \citep{GoldsmithPinkhamSorkinSwift2020}. More recent econometric treatments emphasize conditions under which identification can be obtained from shock variation rather than from the exogeneity of shares, along with practical guidance for inference and diagnostic checks at the shock level \citep{BorusyakHullJaravel2022}. This paper does not propose a new identification strategy; it proposes a different exposure regressor (potentially distance-propagated, task-geometry-based) and tests whether it adds content beyond standard discrete and composition-based baselines under familiar identifying assumptions.


Recent work on generative AI provides transparent, task-level occupation exposure indices that score tasks by model capability and aggregate to the occupation level \citep{EloundouEtAl2024}. Their approach operates at high task granularity, but the exposure signal is categorical (e.g., ordered classes) and aggregated via a fixed linear formula. These can be understood as ad hoc oracle proxies: simple similarity scores between occupation descriptors and technology capabilities. The gap we target is different: we model exposure as a continuous function of proximity in a capability/task space and allow ``nearby'' tasks to receive partial exposure weight under an explicit kernel. Our program asks: what structure should such proxies respect?


A growing literature on the mismatch between linguistic similarity and economic substitutability is relevant to interpreting our findings. Text embeddings capture semantic relatedness---words that appear in similar contexts---but economic substitutability depends on whether workers can actually perform different tasks, whether employers consider credentials interchangeable, and whether institutional factors constrain mobility. The semantic-institutional decomposition directly addresses this gap: semantic distance measures task-content similarity, while institutional distance measures credentialing and training barriers that may operate orthogonally.


Finally, measured technological exposure should be separated from distribution and pass-through. Classic and modern evidence documents that productivity growth and compensation growth can diverge for measurement and institutional reasons \citep{BosworthPerry1994,FleckGlaserSprague2011}. More recent work argues that institutional changes affecting bargaining and rents play a first-order role in wage dynamics \citep{StansburySummers2020}. In our framework, exposure coefficients are interpreted as reduced-form effects conditional on pass-through.


Methodologically, the framework sits alongside propagation approaches that model how shocks diffuse across related units using distance-weighted or network-based dependence structures \citep{Conley1999}. The construction of task geometry is also related to text-based matching approaches that map technologies to occupations using semantic similarity \citep{Webb2020}.




\section{Theory}
\label{sec:theory}


This section defines the theoretical objects, building from primitives to the structural mapping: (i) the task domain as a metric-measure space; (ii) occupations as probability measures over tasks; (iii) the task-space oracle as the ideal structural mapping that binds them; (iv) the semantic-institutional distance decomposition; and (v) propagation mechanisms. Throughout, we develop toy models to build intuition before the empirical implementation in \cref{sec:empirics}.


\subsection{The Task Manifold}
\label{subsec:task-manifold}


We measure task similarity using text embeddings of O*NET work activity descriptions. Activities described in similar language are treated as economically close; activities with dissimilar descriptions are distant. This similarity structure is formalized as a metric space.


\begin{definition}[Task domain]
\label{def:task-domain}
The \emph{task domain} is a compact metric-measure space
\[
\mathcal{T} = (T,d,\mu),
\]
where $T$ is a set of tasks, $d : T \times T \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ is a metric encoding similarity, and $\mu$ is a reference measure for economic mass.
\end{definition}


Distance is the primitive notion of similarity: tasks $x,y$ are close if capabilities and complements transfer at low friction. The framework uses this geometry to encode a prior on spillovers: shocks affect nearby tasks more strongly.


\begin{remark}[Manifold vs Graph]
\label{rem:graph-vs-manifold}
The manifold representation assumes continuous similarity: tasks $x, y$ at small distance $d(x,y)$ are economically interchangeable at low friction. An alternative representation treats activities as discrete nodes in a graph, where adjacency (shared occupation usage) rather than continuous distance determines relatedness. The empirical program tests which representation better predicts economic outcomes.
\end{remark}


\begin{example}[The Task Line]
\label{ex:task-line}
Consider a simple task domain $T = [0, 1]$ representing a single dimension of complexity (e.g., ``manual'' to ``cognitive''). The metric $d(x, y) = |x - y|$ captures the cost of retooling from task $x$ to task $y$. The reference measure $\mu$ is uniform, representing an equal density of economic value across the complexity spectrum.
\end{example}


\begin{assumption}[Admissible task representation]
\label{assump:admissible-representation}
An empirical representation of the task domain is admissible if, for at least one validation target not used in construction (e.g., wage comovement, worker mobility), occupation-pair relatedness in activity space predicts stronger observed relatedness. Specifically:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Metric axioms (if continuous).} The distance $\widehat d$ satisfies the metric axioms on its domain.
    \item \textbf{Scale robustness.} The representation is invariant to irrelevant rescalings of inputs.
    \item \textbf{External diagnostic.} For at least one validation target not used in construction, greater activity-space relatedness predicts stronger observed economic relatedness.
\end{enumerate}
\end{assumption}


\subsection{Occupations as Measures}
\label{subsec:occupations}


Each occupation is represented by a probability distribution over activities, constructed from O*NET importance ratings. The distribution encodes how intensively the occupation uses each activity.


\begin{definition}[Occupations as measures]
\label{def:occupations}
An occupation $j$ is represented by a probability measure $\rho_j$ over $T$ with density $f_j(x) = d\rho_j/d\mu(x)$, interpreted as the intensity with which occupation $j$ uses task $x$.
\end{definition}


\begin{example}[Occupations on the Line (toy illustration)]
\label{ex:occupations-line}
To build intuition, consider two occupations on the task line $T = [0,1]$. ``Occupation A'' (Specialist) is concentrated at the manual end, modeled as a Gaussian measure centered at $x=0.2$ with narrow variance $\sigma_A^2 = 0.01$. ``Occupation B'' (Generalist) is centered at $x=0.8$ but has wider variance $\sigma_B^2 = 0.04$, so its support extends partially into the middle range. The overlap integral $\int \rho_A(x) \rho_B(x)\, d\mu(x)$ quantifies their task-space relatedness. This is intuition only; the empirical implementation uses 2,087 activities and 894 occupations.
\end{example}


\subsection{The Task-Space Oracle}
\label{subsec:oracle}


With the domain and agents defined, we can now define the structural mapping that binds them. The \emph{task-space oracle} represents the ideal object: a complete model that maps task-space representations, shocks, and institutions to equilibrium outcomes.


\begin{definition}[Task-space oracle]
\label{def:oracle}
The \emph{task-space oracle} is a hypothetical complete structural mapping
\[
\mathcal{F}: (\mathcal{T}, \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{I}, \mathcal{M}) \to (\Delta \mathbf{T}, \mathbf{L}, \mathbf{W}, \mathbf{U}),
\]
where the inputs are: $\mathcal{T}$ (task-space representation), $\mathcal{S}$ (technology shock profiles), $\mathcal{I}$ (institutional structure: wage-setting, licensing, bargaining), and $\mathcal{M}$ (equilibrium mechanisms: search, matching, learning). The outputs are: $\Delta \mathbf{T}$ (changes in task structure), $\mathbf{L}$ (employment by occupation), $\mathbf{W}$ (wage distribution), and $\mathbf{U}$ (unemployment).
\end{definition}


\begin{remark}[Scope condition]
\label{rem:why-not-oracle}
The oracle mapping requires correct specification of task-space geometry, shock propagation, worker reallocation dynamics, firm responses, and general-equilibrium feedbacks. Misspecification in any component contaminates the whole. This motivates a modular approach: test each component separately against observed data, then assemble validated modules.
\end{remark}


\begin{remark}[Oracle as organizing metaphor]
\label{rem:oracle-metaphor}
The oracle is an organizing metaphor, not an estimation target. Its value is taxonomic: it clarifies which components a complete model would require, allowing us to test components in isolation. This paper tests two components---task-space geometry and the semantic-institutional decomposition---against observed mobility patterns. We do not claim to estimate or approximate the oracle itself; we validate necessary conditions that candidate representations must satisfy.
\end{remark}


\begin{remark}[Model-space axes]
\label{rem:model-space}
Candidate models for the oracle vary along several axes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Representation axis:} Discrete taxonomies (routine/non-routine) vs.\ continuous manifolds (text embeddings) vs.\ hybrid graph structures (activity co-occurrence networks).
    \item \textbf{Friction axis:} Purely semantic/skill-based frictions vs.\ institutional frictions (licensing, job zones, unions, contracts).
    \item \textbf{Adjustment axis:} Static reduced-form vs.\ dynamic search/learning vs.\ full equilibrium models.
    \item \textbf{Technology axis:} Capability-based exposure (text similarity) vs.\ task-path simulations vs.\ endogenous technology adoption.
\end{enumerate}
This paper focuses on a particular slice: task representation via semantic manifold plus institutional wedge, tested against worker mobility and wage comovement.
\end{remark}


\begin{remark}[Observable projections of $\mathcal{F}$]
\label{rem:projections}
This paper tests three projections of the oracle:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Task similarity structure:} Which activities are described similarly? (From text embeddings.)
    \item \textbf{Worker occupation transitions:} Where do workers go when they switch? (From CPS panel data.)
    \item \textbf{Wage comovement:} Which occupation pairs experience correlated wage changes? (From OES wage data.)
\end{enumerate}
Each projection tests a different aspect of the candidate task-space representation. If the representation is correct, it should be consistent with all three.
\end{remark}


\subsection{The Semantic-Institutional Decomposition}
\label{subsec:decomposition}


A central theoretical contribution is the decomposition of effective distance between occupations into semantic and institutional components. This addresses the problem that semantic similarity (task-content overlap) does not imply economic proximity (ease of worker reallocation).


\begin{definition}[Effective distance decomposition]
\label{def:effective-distance}
Define the \emph{effective distance} between occupations $i$ and $j$ as:
\[
d_{\mathrm{eff}}(i,j) = d_{\mathrm{sem}}(i,j) + \lambda \cdot d_{\mathrm{inst}}(i,j),
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $d_{\mathrm{sem}}(i,j) \in [0,1]$ is \emph{semantic distance}: one minus the normalized kernel-weighted overlap of activity distributions. It measures task-content dissimilarity.
    \item $d_{\mathrm{inst}}(i,j) \geq 0$ is \emph{institutional distance}: the sum of job zone difference and certification barrier difference. It measures credentialing and training dissimilarity.
    \item $\lambda \geq 0$ is the relative weight of institutional vs.\ semantic friction.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}


\begin{remark}[The brain surgeon / butcher problem]
\label{rem:surgeon-butcher}
Consider two occupations: brain surgeon and butcher. Both involve cutting tissue with precision tools. On a purely semantic basis (task descriptions involving cutting, tool use, manual dexterity), they may appear similar. But economically, they are vastly distant: a butcher cannot become a brain surgeon without years of medical training and credentialing. The semantic distance $d_{\mathrm{sem}}$ may be moderate, but the institutional distance $d_{\mathrm{inst}}$ is maximal (job zone 5 vs.\ job zone 2; extensive certification vs.\ minimal). The effective distance correctly identifies them as economically distant despite superficial task similarity.
\end{remark}


\begin{remark}[Testable implications]
\label{rem:decomposition-test}
If $\lambda > 0$ and statistically significant, institutional distance predicts mobility independently of semantic distance. The CPS mobility test (\cref{subsec:cps-mobility}) estimates $\lambda$ from observed transition patterns.
\end{remark}


\begin{remark}[Asymmetry]
\label{rem:asymmetry}
The institutional distance $d_{\mathrm{inst}}$ as defined is symmetric, but the underlying barrier is not: moving \emph{up} job zones requires training; moving \emph{down} does not. A surgeon can become a butcher; the reverse requires medical school. An extension decomposes $d_{\mathrm{inst}}$ into upward and downward components:
\[
d_{\mathrm{inst}}^{\mathrm{up}}(i,j) = \max(0, \mathrm{Zone}_j - \mathrm{Zone}_i), \quad
d_{\mathrm{inst}}^{\mathrm{down}}(i,j) = \max(0, \mathrm{Zone}_i - \mathrm{Zone}_j).
\]
We would expect the coefficient on upward mobility to exceed that on downward mobility.
\end{remark}


\subsection{Technological Fields and Shocks}
\label{subsec:tech-fields}


Technology shifts labor demand through displacement (substitution) and augmentation (complementarity). We represent both as fields over $T$.


\begin{definition}[Technological state]
\label{def:tech-state}
At time $t$, the technological state is a pair of bounded measurable functions
\[
A_t, C_t : T \to \mathbb{R},
\]
where $A_t(x)\ge 0$ is displacement intensity at task $x$ and $C_t(x)>0$ is a complementarity factor at task $x$.
\end{definition}


\begin{definition}[Technological shock profile]
\label{def:shock-field}
Let $I_t \in L^2(T,\mu)$ denote an exogenous innovation input at time $t$.
The profile $I_t(x)$ represents the intensity with which the frontier is pushed at task $x$ by R\&D, investment, or related innovation activity.
\end{definition}


In applications, $I_t$ is constructed and then potentially propagated through a measured geometry. In the prospective evaluation, multiple candidate $I_t$ are compared; the shock mapping is treated as a testable component of measurement rather than as a structural primitive.


\subsection{Propagation of Shocks Across Tasks}
\label{subsec:propagation}


A technology shock to one activity may spill over to similar activities. We model this as a weighted average: each activity's exposure equals a distance-weighted sum of the shock profile, with nearby activities receiving higher weight. The alternative is no spillover: shocks affect only directly-targeted activities.


\begin{definition}[Propagation mechanisms]
\label{def:propagation-mechanisms}
\textbf{(A) Kernel propagation (continuous).} The spillover operator computes a distance-weighted average:
\[
[\widetilde{\mathcal{K}}_d f](x)
=
\frac{\int_T k\bigl(d(x,y)\bigr)\, f(y)\, d\mu(y)}{\int_T k\bigl(d(x,y)\bigr)\, d\mu(y)},
\]
where $k(\cdot)$ is a kernel function that is positive and decreasing in distance.

\textbf{(B) Identity propagation (discrete).} No spillover: $[\mathcal{I} f](x) = f(x)$. Shocks affect only directly-targeted activities.
\end{definition}


Baseline implementation of kernel propagation uses an exponential kernel with a single spillover scale:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exp-kernel}
k\bigl(d(x,y); \sigma\bigr)
=
\exp\!\left(- \frac{d(x,y)}{\sigma}\right),
\qquad
\theta = \sigma > 0.
\end{equation}


\begin{remark}[Bandwidth calibration]
\label{rem:bandwidth-calibration}
A critical methodological finding is that $\sigma$ must be calibrated to the \emph{local} distance structure (median nearest-neighbor distance), not the global distance distribution (median of all pairwise distances). When distances cluster in a narrow band, calibrating $\sigma$ to the global median causes kernel collapse: all weights become approximately equal, destroying the signal. These propagation mechanisms underlie the retrospective tests in \cref{subsec:retrospective-roadmap} and the prospective AI profiles in \cref{subsec:prospective}.
\end{remark}


\subsection{Aggregation to Occupations and Outcome Equations}
\label{subsec:aggregation}


\begin{definition}[Occupation-level exposure functionals]
\label{def:exposure-functionals}
Given $(A_t,C_t)$ and measures $\{\rho_j\}_{j=1}^J$, define
\[
E^{A}_{j,t} \equiv \int_T A_t(x)\, d\rho_j(x),
\qquad
E^{C}_{j,t} \equiv \int_T \log C_t(x)\, d\rho_j(x).
\]
The pipeline is: (i) shock profile $I_t$ on tasks, (ii) propagation $A_t = \mathcal{P} I_t$ across tasks (continuous or discrete), (iii) aggregation via $\rho_j$ to occupation-level exposure.
\end{definition}


\begin{definition}[Discrete exposure]
\label{def:discrete-exposure}
Under identity propagation ($\mathcal{P} = \mathcal{I}$), occupation-level exposure reduces to:
\[
E^A_j = \sum_{a \in T_n} I(a) \cdot \rho_j(a),
\]
which is a weighted sum of shock intensity over the occupation's activity distribution.
\end{definition}


\begin{definition}[Reduced-form outcome equations (partial equilibrium)]
\label{def:reduced-form}
Let $w_{j,t}$ and $L_{j,t}$ denote wages and employment. We evaluate
\[
\Delta \log w_{j,t} = \beta_w E^{A}_{j,t} + \gamma_w E^{C}_{j,t} + \bm{X}_{j,t}'\delta_w + u_{j,t},
\]
\[
\Delta \log L_{j,t} = \beta_L E^{A}_{j,t} + \gamma_L E^{C}_{j,t} + \bm{X}_{j,t}'\delta_L + v_{j,t},
\]
interpreting coefficients as conditional associations used to compare exposure measures.
\end{definition}


\begin{remark}[Implied covariance structure---diagnostic target]
\label{rem:covariance}
If outcomes load on exposure with common sign and shocks are spatially concentrated in task space, then occupation pairs with greater task-distribution overlap should exhibit stronger outcome comovement.

\textbf{Kernel-weighted overlap.} Define occupation-pair proximity as
\[
\mathrm{Overlap}_K(i,j) = \int_T \int_T \rho_i(x)\, \rho_j(y)\, k(d(x,y); \sigma)\, d\mu(x)\, d\mu(y).
\]

\textbf{Normalized overlap.} To control for concentration effects (specialist vs generalist occupations), define normalized overlap:
\[
\mathrm{NormOverlap}_K(i,j) = \frac{\mathrm{Overlap}_K(i,j)}{\sqrt{\mathrm{Overlap}_K(i,i) \cdot \mathrm{Overlap}_K(j,j)}}.
\]

\textbf{Binary overlap.} Define binary overlap as the Jaccard index:
\[
\mathrm{Overlap}_B(i,j) = \frac{|A_i \cap A_j|}{|A_i \cup A_j|},
\]
where $A_j = \{a : \rho_j(a) > 0\}$ is the activity support of occupation $j$.
\end{remark}


\subsection{Wage-setting wedge and pass-through (scope condition)}
\label{subsec:wedge}


Observed wages need not track marginal products one-for-one. We record this as a scope condition rather than a mechanism to be estimated.


\begin{definition}[Occupation-level wage-setting wedge]
\label{def:wedge}
Let $\mathrm{MPL}_{j,t}$ denote the (unobserved) marginal product of labor for occupation $j$. Define $b_{j,t} > 0$ such that
\[
\log w_{j,t} = \log \mathrm{MPL}_{j,t} + \log b_{j,t}.
\]
\end{definition}


Empirically, proxies for wage-setting environments (e.g., union coverage, licensing prevalence, concentration) enter as controls or descriptive correlates. Interpretation of exposure coefficients is conditional on these environments \citep{StansburySummers2020,AutorSalomons2018}.


\subsection{New tasks and an evolving task domain (extension)}
\label{subsec:newtasks}


The baseline holds $(T,\mu)$ fixed. Over longer horizons, outcomes depend on task creation and reweighting \citep{AcemogluRestrepo2018}. This section records extension paths: expanding $T_t$, time-varying $\mu_t$, and time-varying occupation measures $\rho_{j,t}$.




\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:empirics}


This section operationalizes the theoretical framework using O*NET data and tests it against observed labor market outcomes. We describe how to (i) construct a discrete approximation of the task manifold, (ii) build occupation measures from ratings, (iii) compute activity distances, and (iv) evaluate the resulting exposures against baselines. The tests are organized as stress tests of candidate oracle modules.


\subsection{Empirical Strategy as Oracle Module Testing}
\label{subsec:strategy}


Each empirical test in this section is a stress test of a specific component of a future task-space oracle:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Primary test: CPS worker mobility (\cref{subsec:cps-mobility}).} Tests whether the candidate effective distance $d_{\mathrm{eff}} = d_{\mathrm{sem}} + \lambda \cdot d_{\mathrm{inst}}$ predicts observed destination choice. If transitions are less frequently observed to high-$d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ destinations, the representation has predictive validity for mobility.
    
    \item \textbf{Complementary test: Wage comovement (\cref{subsec:wage-comovement}).} Tests whether the same semantic geometry has interpretable equilibrium wage implications. If occupation pairs with high overlap experience correlated wage changes, the geometry captures economically relevant structure.
    
    \item \textbf{Prospective test: AI shock profiles (\cref{subsec:prospective}).} Tests whether the framework can be applied to novel shocks (generative AI) with pre-specified candidate profiles.
\end{enumerate}

The CPS mobility test is primary because it directly tests revealed preference over occupation pairs at the worker level, avoiding ecological inference problems and providing the cleanest test of the semantic-institutional decomposition.


\subsection{Discrete Approximation of the Manifold}
\label{subsec:discrete-approx}


We use O*NET Detailed Work Activities (DWAs) as the task unit. O*NET provides 2,087 DWAs linked to 894 occupations via task statements. Each occupation's activity distribution is constructed from importance ratings.


\begin{definition}[Empirical activity domain]
\label{def:empirical-activity-domain}
Let $T_n = \{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ denote the set of DWAs ($n = 2{,}087$). The reference measure $\mu$ is uniform. The metric $d$ is constructed from text embeddings (\cref{subsubsec:recipes}).
\end{definition}


\begin{remark}[Constructing occupation measures from O*NET]
\label{rem:rho-construction}
The occupation measure $\rho_j$ is constructed from O*NET ratings via the following steps \citep{onet2024dwa,onet2024scales}:


\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(A) Raw weight construction $w_j(a)$:}


\emph{If $T_n = $ GWA (Work Activities file):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Retrieve Importance $\mathrm{IM}_{j,a} \in [1,5]$ and Level $\mathrm{LV}_{j,a} \in [0,7]$ for each occupation-activity pair.
    \item Baseline: $w_j(a) = \mathrm{IM}_{j,a} \times \mathrm{LV}_{j,a}$ after rescaling both to $[0,1]$.
\end{itemize}


\emph{If $T_n = $ DWA (Detailed Work Activities):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item DWAs lack direct importance ratings; importance is derived via linked tasks.
    \item Let $\mathrm{Tasks}(j, a) = \{\text{tasks in occupation } j \text{ linked to DWA } a\}$.
    \item Baseline aggregator: $w_j(a) = \max_{t \in \mathrm{Tasks}(j,a)} \mathrm{IM}_{j,t}$ (top importance).
\end{itemize}


\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(B) Suppression and missingness rules:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Set $w_j(a) = 0$ if O*NET flags ``Recommend Suppress $=$ Y'' for that cell.
    \item Set $w_j(a) = 0$ if ``Not Relevant $=$ Y'' (per O*NET's 75\% rule for Level).
    \item If an occupation has fewer than 10 activities with nonzero weight after filtering, flag the occupation.
\end{itemize}


\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(C) Normalization to probability measure:}
\[
\rho_j(a) = \frac{w_j(a)}{\sum_{a' \in T_n} w_j(a')}.
\]
This yields $\rho_j \in \mathcal{P}(T_n)$.


\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(D) Implementation summary (O*NET 30.0):}

The baseline implementation uses:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Files: Task Ratings.xlsx (task importance by occupation), Tasks to DWAs.xlsx (task-to-DWA linkages), DWA Reference.xlsx (DWA definitions).
    \item Coverage: 894 occupations with valid measures after filtering.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(E) Distribution statistics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Sparsity: 99.1\% of occupation-activity weights are zero.
    \item Median activities per occupation: 19 (of 2,087 possible).
    \item Median pairwise Jaccard overlap: 0.0 (most occupation pairs share no activities).
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}


\begin{remark}[Matrix representation of the spillover operator]
\label{rem:matrix-notation}
In discrete instantiation with uniform $\mu$, the spillover operator becomes a matrix $K \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$:
\[
K_{ab} = k(d(a,b); \sigma) = \exp(-d(a,b)/\sigma).
\]
Note: we do \emph{not} row-normalize $K$ in the baseline implementation.
\end{remark}


\subsubsection{Construction recipes for the activity metric}
\label{subsubsec:recipes}


\begin{remark}[Recipes for constructing $\widehat d$ on activities]
\label{rem:recipes}
Instantiate $T$ as a finite set of activities (GWA or DWA). Occupations are probability measures over activities, not points in $T$.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Recipe Y (text-embedding geometry).} Embed activity titles/descriptions using a sentence encoder (all-mpnet-base-v2, 768 dimensions). Define $\widehat d(a,b)$ as cosine distance in embedding space.
    
    \emph{Validation targets:} mobility, wage comovement.
    
    \item \textbf{Binary Jaccard (no distance).} Define activity-space relatedness via set intersection without any distance weighting:
    \[
    \mathrm{Jaccard}(i,j) = \frac{|A_i \cap A_j|}{|A_i \cup A_j|},
    \]
    where $A_j = \{a : \rho_j(a) > 0\}$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\textbf{Measurement error correction.} Raw CPS occupation switching rates ($\sim$6.7\% monthly) substantially exceed true mobility rates ($\sim$0.5--1.0\% per Kambourov \& Manovskii 2008). The discrepancy arises from coding error: self-reported occupation varies across interviews even when the underlying job is unchanged.

We apply a \emph{persistence filter}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Origin stability:} OCC$(t-2)$ = OCC$(t-1)$ (worker was in origin for at least one prior period)
    \item \textbf{Transition occurred:} OCC$(t) \neq$ OCC$(t-1)$
    \item \textbf{Destination persistence:} OCC$(t)$ = OCC$(t+1)$ = OCC$(t+2)$ (worker remains in destination)
\end{enumerate}
This removes spurious transitions where occupation codes fluctuate due to reporting inconsistency. Retention rate (67.3\%) is consistent with literature estimates that 25--50\% of raw CPS occupation changes are measurement error.


\textbf{Crosswalk.} Mapping path: Census 2010 (OCC2010) $\to$ 6-digit SOC $\to$ O*NET-SOC 2019. Coverage: 882 of 894 O*NET occupations matched (98.7\%). When multiple O*NET codes map to one Census code, distances are averaged.


\subsubsection{Distance Matrix Construction}
\label{subsubsec:distance-construction}


\textbf{Semantic distance ($d_{\mathrm{sem}}$).} Constructed as normalized kernel overlap converted to distance:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Embed 2,087 DWAs using MPNet (all-mpnet-base-v2, 768 dimensions)
    \item Compute cosine distance matrix $D_{\mathrm{act}}$ over activities
    \item Calibrate kernel bandwidth: $\sigma$ = median nearest-neighbor distance = 0.0096
    \item Build kernel matrix: $K_{ab} = \exp(-D_{\mathrm{act}}(a,b)/\sigma)$
    \item Load occupation-activity importance matrix (894 $\times$ 2,087)
    \item Row-normalize to probability measures: $\rho_j(a) = w_j(a) / \sum_a w_j(a)$
    \item Compute normalized overlap: $O_{ij} = \frac{\sum_{a,b} \rho_i(a) K_{ab} \rho_j(b)}{\sqrt{\sum_{a,b} \rho_i(a) K_{ab} \rho_i(b)} \cdot \sqrt{\sum_{a,b} \rho_j(a) K_{ab} \rho_j(b)}}$
    \item Convert to distance: $d_{\mathrm{sem}}(i,j) = 1 - O_{ij}$
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Conditional Logit Specification}
\label{subsubsec:clogit}


\textbf{Framework:} McFadden's conditional logit for discrete choice.

For worker $k$ who switches from origin occupation $i$, the utility of destination $j$ is:
\[
U_{kj} = \alpha \cdot (-d_{\mathrm{sem}}(i,j)) + \beta \cdot (-d_{\mathrm{inst}}(i,j)) + \varepsilon_{kj}
\]

Distances enter negatively: the model predicts transitions are more likely to destinations with \textbf{lower} semantic and institutional distance from the origin.

\textbf{Choice probability:}
\[
P(j | i, \text{switch}) = \frac{\exp(\alpha \cdot (-d_{\mathrm{sem}}(i,j)) + \beta \cdot (-d_{\mathrm{inst}}(i,j)))}{\sum_{j' \in J} \exp(\alpha \cdot (-d_{\mathrm{sem}}(i,j')) + \beta \cdot (-d_{\mathrm{inst}}(i,j')))}
\]

\textbf{Choice set construction.} Full choice sets (all 447 Census occupations) are computationally prohibitive. Following standard practice, we use random sampling of alternatives: for each observed transition (origin $i \to$ chosen destination $j$), include the chosen destination ($Y = 1$) and sample $K = 10$ non-chosen alternatives uniformly at random ($Y = 0$). This yields 11 observations per transition, grouped by transition ID. Under IIA, random sampling of alternatives yields consistent coefficient estimates.

\textbf{Sample size:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Observed transitions: 89,329
    \item Rows per transition: 11
    \item Total choice-set observations: 982,619
\end{itemize}

Both coefficients are positive (recall covariates are negated distances), indicating transitions are more frequently observed to destinations that are: (1) semantically similar to the origin (higher task overlap), and (2) institutionally similar to the origin (similar job zone and certification requirements).


\textbf{Statistical significance.} Both coefficients are estimated with high precision: $\alpha$: $t = 98.5$; $\beta$: $t = 63.4$. The large $t$-statistics primarily reflect sample size (89,329 transitions). Statistical significance at this scale conveys little information about economic importance; we therefore focus interpretation on effect magnitudes.


\textbf{Economic magnitude---odds ratio interpretation.}

For a 1-unit increase in $d_{\mathrm{sem}}$ (moving to a more semantically distant occupation):
\[
\text{Odds ratio} = \exp(-\alpha) = \exp(-2.994) = 0.050
\]
A 1-unit increase in semantic distance is associated with 95\% lower odds of observing that destination choice.

For a 1-unit increase in $d_{\mathrm{inst}}$ (moving to a more institutionally distant occupation):
\[
\text{Odds ratio} = \exp(-\beta) = \exp(-0.215) = 0.807
\]
A 1-unit increase in institutional distance is associated with 19.3\% lower odds of observing that destination choice.


\textbf{Standardized comparison.} The semantic coefficient is 13.9$\times$ larger than the institutional coefficient ($\alpha/\beta = 13.9$). However, the scales differ: $d_{\mathrm{sem}}$ range $\approx 0.9$; $d_{\mathrm{inst}}$ range $\approx 3.6$.

On a standardized basis (using sample standard deviations):
\begin{itemize}
    \item 1-SD increase in $d_{\mathrm{sem}}$ (0.069): odds ratio $= \exp(-2.994 \times 0.069) = 0.814$
    \item 1-SD increase in $d_{\mathrm{inst}}$ (1.35): odds ratio $= \exp(-0.215 \times 1.35) = 0.748$
\end{itemize}

On a standardized basis, the two effects are comparable in magnitude. The difference is modest and potentially sensitive to measurement choices; the key finding is that both components are independently predictive, not that one dominates.


\textbf{Decomposition test.} The key question: Does institutional distance add predictive value beyond semantic distance?

Result: $\beta = 0.215$, $t = 63.42$, $p < 10^{-100}$.

\textbf{Conclusion:} Institutional distance is a significant predictor conditional on semantic distance. Transitions are observed less frequently to destinations with larger job zone gaps and certification barriers, even controlling for task-content similarity. Both semantic and institutional structure independently predict observed mobility patterns; they are not redundant ($r = 0.36$).


\subsubsection{Interpretation}
\label{subsubsec:cps-interpretation}


\textbf{Consistency with the decomposition.} The results are consistent with the effective distance decomposition (\cref{def:effective-distance}): both $\alpha > 0$ and $\beta > 0$, indicating transitions are less frequently observed to destinations that are either semantically or institutionally distant. The two measures are not redundant ($r = 0.36$).


\textbf{Interpretive hypotheses.} If the associations reflect causal mechanisms, the two components would capture distinct barriers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Semantic distance} ($\alpha = 2.994$): Skill transferability, learning costs, productivity gaps.
    \item \textbf{Institutional distance} ($\beta = 0.215$): Hiring barriers, licensing requirements, training investments.
\end{enumerate}
We cannot distinguish these mechanisms from confounds (e.g., unobserved worker heterogeneity, employer preferences) with the current design.


\textbf{Limitations.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{IIA assumption:} The conditional logit assumes Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives. This assumption is implausible for occupation choice, where adding or removing options should affect relative probabilities of similar occupations. Nested logit or mixed logit specifications would relax this assumption; we retain conditional logit for tractability and comparability with standard practice.
    \item \textbf{Cosine distance is not a proper metric:} Cosine distance in embedding space does not satisfy the triangle inequality. The theoretical framework (\cref{def:task-domain}) assumes a metric space. We proceed as if triangle inequality violations are empirically small, but this is a maintained assumption.
    \item \textbf{Panel weights not used:} Estimates are unweighted. Weighted estimation may change point estimates.
    \item \textbf{Choice set sampling:} Random 10:1 sampling of alternatives may introduce bias if the true choice set is systematically different.
    \item \textbf{Certification measure coverage:} Only 61.1\% of occupations have direct certification data; 38.9\% are imputed with the median. This attenuates $\beta$ toward zero; the true institutional effect may be larger.
    \item \textbf{Time period inconsistency:} Distances are computed from 2024 O*NET data but applied to 2015--2024 transitions. Task content and credential requirements may have shifted.
    \item \textbf{Selection on switching:} We observe transitions conditional on switching. Workers who would switch but face prohibitive barriers remain in their origin occupation and are not in the sample.
    \item \textbf{O*NET as constructed perception:} O*NET ratings reflect analyst judgments and incumbent worker surveys, not objective task measurement. The ``task space'' is a constructed representation, not ground truth.
\end{enumerate}


\textbf{Implications for exposure measurement.} If the decomposition is correct, any task-space exposure measure should incorporate both semantic and institutional distance. A model that predicts workers will flow to semantically similar occupations without accounting for institutional barriers would overstate reallocation to credential-protected occupations. This is a conditional claim: it holds if the decomposition reflects real mobility frictions, not merely data artifacts.

\begin{itemize}
    \item CPS mobility: worker-level association between distances and destination choice
    \item Wage comovement: occupation-pair correlation in wage changes
\end{itemize}
Both suggest the semantic geometry captures structure correlated with economic outcomes. The CPS test additionally shows that institutional distance is predictive conditional on semantic distance.

\subsection{Prospective Evaluation: Generative AI}
\label{subsec:prospective}


The prospective evaluation applies the framework to generative AI. The object is comparison among pre-specified shock profiles, not identification of a single ``true'' profile.


\subsubsection{Candidate Shock Profiles}


Each candidate defines $I^{(v)}(a)$ for activities $a \in T_n$.


\medskip
\noindent\textbf{$v_1$ (capability-only):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Construction: Score each activity on ``language/cognitive capability relevance'' using a rule-based mapping.
    \item Positive loading on activities tagged as involving information processing, written comprehension, oral expression; negative loading on physical/manual activities.
\end{itemize}


\noindent\textbf{$v_2$ (capability $+$ structure):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Construction: $v_1$ loadings plus additional positive weight on activities tagged as structured/repetitive.
    \item ``Structured'' operationalized via O*NET ``Structured vs Unstructured'' rating.
    \item Hypothesis: structure amplifies AI exposure beyond raw capability.
\end{itemize}


\noindent\textbf{$v_3$ (external anchor---PRIMARY):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Construction: Derive from an independent source not using O*NET geometry.
    \item Options: (a) Human expert ratings; (b) Published rubric mapping (Eloundou et al.\ 2024); (c) Patent-activity matching.
    \item Designated as primary profile ex ante.
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{Profile Selection Protocol}


\noindent\textit{Primary-profile approach (baseline):}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Designate $v_3$ as primary profile ex ante.
    \item $v_1$ and $v_2$ are robustness checks and hypothesis tests.
    \item No profile selection based on outcome fit.
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{Implications for AI-Based Exposure Measures}


The CPS mobility findings have direct implications for how AI shock profiles should be deployed:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Any task-space oracle must incorporate institutional distance.} Semantic similarity alone overstates the feasibility of worker reallocation. A model that predicts workers displaced by AI will flow to semantically similar occupations without accounting for credentialing barriers will systematically mispredict adjustment paths.
    
    \item \textbf{AI exposure profiles should distinguish ``can do'' from ``allowed to do.''} If generative AI displaces tasks in high-credential occupations (e.g., legal research, medical documentation), workers may face institutional barriers to moving into other occupations even if their semantic skills transfer.
    
    \item \textbf{Counterfactual reallocation simulations should respect $d_{\mathrm{eff}}$.} When simulating how workers will adjust to AI shocks, the oracle should weight destination occupations by $\exp(-d_{\mathrm{eff}})$, not just $\exp(-d_{\mathrm{sem}})$.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Model Evaluation Criteria}
\label{subsec:evaluation}


The framework is evaluated against simple baselines using explicit criteria.


\medskip
\noindent\textbf{PRIMARY CRITERIA (all must be addressed):}


\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Incremental explanatory power.} Activity-based exposure should improve fit relative to discrete baselines by an economically meaningful margin.
    
    \item \textbf{Parameter stability.} Results should be qualitatively stable across domain choices (DWA vs GWA) and weighting schemes.
    
    \item \textbf{Non-redundancy.} We target low-to-moderate correlation between semantic and institutional distance. In the baseline implementation we obtain $r \approx 0.36$, indicating related but non-redundant structure.
    
    \item \textbf{Non-tautological validation.} Geometry diagnostics must use targets not used in construction.
\end{enumerate}


\medskip
\noindent\textbf{REVISION PROTOCOL:}


If primary criteria fail:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Do not add auxiliary structure to rescue fit.
    \item Report null result: ``activity-based measure does not outperform discrete baselines under tested specifications.''
\end{itemize}


\section{Extensions, Limitations, and Historical Regimes}
\label{sec:extensions}


This paper targets a compact, implementable measurement pipeline. Extensions are recorded here for future work.


\subsection{Dynamic accumulation and reversals}
\label{subsec:dynamic}


The baseline construction is static. For settings where shock accumulation or reversal matters, define:
\[
A_t = \delta A_{t-1} + I_t + \varepsilon_t,
\]
where $\delta \in [0,1]$ governs persistence.


\subsection{Asymmetric institutional distance}
\label{subsec:asymmetric}


The current $d_{\mathrm{inst}}$ is symmetric, but upward mobility (into higher job zones) is harder than downward. An extension decomposes:
\[
d_{\mathrm{inst}}^{\mathrm{up}}(i,j) = \max(0, \mathrm{Zone}_j - \mathrm{Zone}_i), \quad
d_{\mathrm{inst}}^{\mathrm{down}}(i,j) = \max(0, \mathrm{Zone}_i - \mathrm{Zone}_j).
\]
We would expect $\beta^{\mathrm{up}} > \beta^{\mathrm{down}}$.

\label{subsec:augmentation}


The framework carries $C_t$ (complementarity field) and $E^C$ (augmentation exposure) for symmetry. Estimation requires task-level productivity complementarities not available in O*NET.


\subsection{Supervised dimension extraction}

An alternative to unsupervised text embeddings is supervised extraction of economically-relevant dimensions. Given theoretical priors about dimensions that matter (routine/non-routine, cognitive/manual), one can train linear probes to identify concept directions and project all activities onto these dimensions.

An extension would model transition \emph{rates}: do occupation pairs with lower $d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ have higher observed transition probabilities? This tests whether the geometry predicts the \emph{likelihood} of switching, not just the direction conditional on switching.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}


This paper develops a geometric measurement framework for labor market exposure to technological shocks. The task-space oracle serves as an organizing metaphor---a hypothetical complete structural mapping from task representations, technology shocks, and institutions to equilibrium outcomes. We do not estimate or approximate the oracle; we test candidate components against observed data.


The central theoretical contribution is the effective distance decomposition (\cref{def:effective-distance}), separating semantic distance (task-content similarity) from institutional distance (credentialing barriers).


The primary empirical finding is descriptive: in CPS occupation transitions, both semantic distance and institutional distance are significant predictors of observed destination choice. The two measures are correlated at $r = 0.36$, indicating related but non-redundant structure. On a standardized basis, the effects are comparable in magnitude. We interpret this as evidence that both task-content similarity and credentialing barriers are associated with mobility patterns, though we cannot establish causation from this correlational design.


A complementary test using wage comovement shows that semantic overlap is statistically detectable as a predictor of occupation-pair wage correlation, though the $R^2$ values are small (0.005 vs 0.002 for the binary baseline). The value is discriminating between representations, not explaining wage dynamics.


If the decomposition reflects real mobility frictions, it has implications for exposure measurement: any representation that ignores institutional barriers would overstate reallocation feasibility. Future work should address the limitations noted in Section 4---particularly the IIA assumption, the cosine distance metric violation, and the need for two-way clustering in wage comovement tests---before drawing stronger conclusions.

\section{Retrospective Battery: Technical Specifications}
\label{app:retrospective}


This appendix provides full technical specifications for the retrospective diagnostic battery (Tests A, B, C), which are retained as robustness modules.


\subsection{Test A: Task Composition Shifts (ALM-Informed)}


\textbf{Period:} 1980--2000 (or 1980--1998 to match \citet{AutorLevyMurnane2003} exactly).

\textbf{Core question:} When computerization enters industries, do task composition shifts follow continuous distance from automated tasks, or only binary routine/non-routine classification?

\textbf{Design:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Unit of analysis: Industry $\times$ decade
    \item Treatment: Computer capital intensity or computer use (CPS October supplements)
    \item Outcome: Change in employment-weighted task intensity by industry
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Key data requirements:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item ALM task measures (DOT 1977 based) or reconstruction via O*NET mapping
    \item Industry-level computer capital/use: BEA capital data, CPS computer use supplements
    \item Industry $\times$ occupation employment matrices: Census 1980, 1990, 2000
    \item Crosswalk: DOT 1977 $\to$ Census occupation $\to$ occ1990dd $\to$ O*NET-SOC $\to$ DWA embeddings
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Test B: Employment Reallocation (Autor-Dorn Setting)}


\textbf{Period:} 1980--2005 (matches \citet{AutorDorn2013} exactly).

\textbf{Core question:} Do commuting zones with higher continuous routine exposure experience different employment polarization patterns than those with equivalent discrete routine share?

\textbf{Design:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Unit of analysis: Commuting zone ($n = 722$)
    \item Treatment (1980 baseline): Discrete RSH vs.\ continuous CSH
    \item Outcomes: $\Delta$ employment share in service occupations, $\Delta$ employment share in routine occupations, wage changes at percentiles
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Decomposition test:}
\begin{equation}
\Delta Y_{jst} = \delta_t + \beta_1 \times \text{RSH}_{j,t_0} + \beta_3 \times (\text{CSH} - \text{RSH})_{j,t_0} + X'_{j,t_0} \gamma + \phi_s + \varepsilon_{jst}
\end{equation}

If $\beta_3 > 0$ and significant, continuous captures information discrete misses.

\textbf{Replication data sources:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Autor-Dorn replication archive: \texttt{ddorn.net/data.htm}, File [P2]
    \item Crosswalk files [A1]--[A8]: Census occupation $\to$ occ1990dd (330 balanced codes)
    \item Geographic crosswalks [E1]--[E7]: PUMA/County Group $\to$ CZ with \texttt{afactor} weights
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Test C: Robot Displacement (Acemoglu-Restrepo Setting)}


\textbf{Period:} 1990--2007 (or 1993--2014 depending on IFR data availability).

\textbf{Core question:} Does continuous task overlap with robot-exposed activities predict employment/wage changes better than binary robot exposure classification?

\textbf{Design:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Unit of analysis: Commuting zone or occupation
    \item Treatment: Industry-level robot exposure (A-R) vs.\ task-level robot exposure (ours)
    \item Outcomes: Employment-to-population ratio, log wages
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Data sources:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item IFR robot data by industry $\times$ country (1993--2014)
    \item A-R replication files
    \item European robot adoption for IV
\end{itemize}
